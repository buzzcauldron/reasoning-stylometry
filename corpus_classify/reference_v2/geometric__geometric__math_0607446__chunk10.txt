Recall that Hermite interpolation is a generalization of Lagrange interpolation in which one computes a polyn omial that matches not only the values but also some derivatives of a function at some speciﬁed points. More precisely, suppose we are given f ∈ C∞ ([a, b ]), distinct points t1, . . . , t m ∈ [a, b ], and positive integers k1, . . . , k m. We wish to ﬁnd a polynomial of degree less than D = k1 + k2 + · · ·+ km such that for 1 ≤ i ≤ m and 0 ≤ k < k i, p(k)(ti) = f (k)(ti). In other words, p and f agree to order ki at ti. A unique such polynomial always exists, because the linear map that takes a polynomial p of degree less than D to ( p(t1), p ′(t1), . . . , p (k1− 1)(t1), p (t2), p ′(t2), . . . , p (km− 1)(tm) ) is injective (and hence surjective as well).
