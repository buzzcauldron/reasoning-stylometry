The traditional sampling method in process control systems is based on a periodic task model. This is because controllers are executed in a strictly periodic manner. Sensors sample the process data and send it periodically to the appropriate controllers through a communication system such as the fieldbus. Since the fieldbus is shared by multiple sensors, there is some delay between the sampling and control actions. In order to minimize the control loop latency, a higher than necessary sampling frequency is typically adopted, which results in unnecessary waste of energy. We propose a sampling interval control algorithm for tackling this problem. Sampling tasks are not periodic but have both maximum and minimum distance constraints. We shall then discuss the jitter property of sampling schemes under this model and propose algorithms for controlling the sampling intervals of sensors in terms of the problem we shall introduce. Though this problem is NP-hard in general, even for the special case of unit-time tasks, we show how to reduce it to well-studied scheduling models such as periodic models and pinwheel models, at the expense of some loss of schedulability. These reductions allow us to derive efficient schedulability tests that can be used to solve the sampling interval control problem in practice.

Definition: A MinMax task set T is a set of MinMax tasks. Each task is a 3-tuple, consisting of an execution time, a minimum separation time, and a maximum distance. Each task consists of an infinite sequence of jobs. For two consecutive jobs, it is required that the gap between the start of the later job and the finish of the earlier job is at least the minimum separation, and that the span from the start of the earlier job to the finish of the later job is at most the maximum distance.

We could have defined the maximum constraint in terms of start times alone, or finish times alone. However, we think our definition is more germane in practice, i.e., two consecutive jobs are completely within the maximum distance of each other. Besides, these definitions are readily convertible to each other. Similar reasoning applies to our definition of the minimum separation. Note that in our definition, the start time is not the ready time of a job but the time when it is actually executed, and the finish time is the point at which the last part of the job is executed when it completes. This is important when we consider both types of constraints together, otherwise the minimum constraint would be meaningless.

A jitter constraint task set is a set of tasks, each a 3-tuple of execution time, maximum jitter, and maximum distance, such that for two consecutive jobs, the maximum distance constraint holds and the maximum and minimum gaps between consecutive finish times do not differ by more than the jitter bound.

Theorem: If there exists a schedule for a MinMax instance, then there exists a schedule for a corresponding jitter constraint instance, where the jitter bound equals the maximum distance minus the minimum separation minus twice the execution time.

Proof sketch: For a valid MinMax schedule, the maximum gap between consecutive finish times happens when the earlier job is run immediately to finish and the later job is finished at the maximum distance later, which is the maximum distance minus the execution time. The minimum such gap happens when the two jobs are separated by the minimum separation and the later job runs immediately to finish, which is the minimum separation plus the execution time. So the jitter is no more than the difference between these two quantities, which equals the maximum distance minus the minimum separation minus twice the execution time.

This theorem shows that any valid schedule of a MinMax task set also provides a certain degree of jitter guarantee. Note the converse is not true. A valid schedule for a single jitter constraint task could have this task run on the processor continuously without break, in which case the job separation is zero and the jitter is zero, so no minimum separation could be guaranteed.

In this paper we focus on the special case where all execution times are one. We call this unit-time MinMax. In a discrete time model, the granularity of time measurements is in terms of integral multiples of some basic time quantum, and time intervals are always measured between integral time points. No task period can span a time interval that starts in the middle of a time slot and ends in the middle of another one.

We shall now prove that the schedulability decision problem of unit-time MinMax is NP-hard by showing that it is NP-complete. It is easy to see that the problem is in NP. In order to prove its NP-completeness, we reduce a general multi-level unit-time job scheduling problem with temporal distance constraints to it. The general problem can be described as follows: given a set of jobs, each with execution time, ready time, and deadline, the job set is divided into chains of jobs, with head jobs and tail jobs following each head. The objective is to find a schedule meeting the temporal distance constraint requirement that each tail job must be started within the maximum distance of its immediate predecessor. It is straightforward to see that this general problem is only a special case of unit-time MinMax with minimum separation equal to zero. Since the general problem has been proved NP-complete in the strong sense, unit-time MinMax is also NP-complete in the strong sense.

By definition, the pinwheel problem is a special case of the unit-time MinMax problem. By following the existence proof of a cyclic pinwheel schedule, we can also claim the existence of a cyclic schedule for unit-time MinMax: if there exists a schedule for a unit-time MinMax instance, then it has a cyclic schedule whose cycle length is no greater than the product of all the maximum distances.

We first reduce the MinMax problem to a periodic model. The relationship can be expressed by three inequalities relating the period and deadline of each task to its maximum distance and minimum separation. It is straightforward to arrive at the conclusion that for a MinMax task set and any periodic task set whose parameters satisfy these relationships, any valid schedule for the periodic task set is also a valid schedule for the MinMax set. Note the converse is not true, as MinMax allows jobs to drift away from the multiples of a period.

For the first reduction, the answer is straightforward: we are trying to enlarge the deadline, and the largest possible deadline is half the difference between the maximum distance and the minimum separation, rounded up. Then, as long as the resulting utilization sum does not exceed one and the deadline does not exceed the period, the task set will be schedulable by earliest-deadline-first. This reduction process takes linear time.

We are only able to apply the second theorem to unit-time MinMax reduction. We can first check whether a certain equation is satisfied; if so, the period and deadline can be computed, otherwise there is no need to continue. For the remaining inequality, it can first be assumed that a certain quantity is fixed. Since the execution time is one, we need to minimize a particular ratio to make the schedule more likely to succeed. By intuition, both the deadline and the period should be maximized. However, since their sum is bounded by the maximum distance, we need to consider which should be bigger. This involves comparing two candidate ratios, and by sorting tasks in nondecreasing order of deadline, it can be shown which ordering dominates. Based on this deduction, the reduction process also takes linear time.

We also can apply the more-less scheduling model to solve the MinMax problem. First we need to verify whether the MinMax problem satisfies the more-less pre-conditions. Then we can apply the more-less principle to compute the period and deadline for each task. Finally we have to make sure whether the resulting difference between period and deadline is at least the minimum separation. If yes, we can find a solution; otherwise it fails. The reduction only adds a check procedure at the end, which takes linear complexity.

In this section we discuss how to apply the pinwheel model to solve the unit-time MinMax problem. Since pinwheel does not consider minimum separation constraints, it appears difficult to satisfy MinMax with a pinwheel schedule. Fortunately, an observation on pinwheel algorithms yields nice properties we can exploit. In particular, we are able to derive a lower separation bound of a pinwheel schedule. We first use this separation bound as a sufficient condition for making a schedulability decision, and once it is met, we proceed with the deduction, whose success guarantees a valid schedule for the unit-time MinMax set.

Given a pinwheel instance, we only need to check whether a certain distance quantity is no smaller than the minimum separation for each task. We shall first derive the minimum distance separation for pinwheel schedulers. It can be calculated directly from the algorithm: given a pinwheel instance sorted in increasing order, if it can be scheduled by the single-integer reduction, then we can find a schedule such that for a task with a given frequency, the distance separation in the schedule is exactly a specific power-of-two multiple of the reduction base.

Based on this corollary, we can claim a sufficient condition of schedulability for unit-time MinMax: if the sum of the reciprocals of the maximum distances is within the density bound, and the ratio of maximum distance to minimum separation is at least two for every task, then there exists a schedule for it. Furthermore, this ratio bound of two cannot be improved: suppose there exists a smaller real bound; then we can always construct a counterexample task set that is not schedulable while its ratio approaches that smaller bound arbitrarily closely, so no such smaller bound exists.

For a pinwheel instance with two distinct integers, we can find a cyclic pinwheel schedule satisfying certain tight distance ranges for tasks of each frequency. The proof of the tightness of this bound proceeds by direct calculation using known floor and ceiling inequalities, and a simple two-task example demonstrates that the bound cannot be improved.

The above result is not always satisfactory, because the resulting lower bound may be unnecessarily small. We hope to make the distance range narrower, which can be accomplished by padding the schedule with empty tasks. Given two frequencies and task counts satisfying certain nonnegativity conditions, there exist enlarged task counts, at least as large as the originals, such that a certain balance quantity falls in a bounded range. Without loss of generality, assuming the smaller of the two frequencies serves as the bound, if the balance quantity computed from the original counts already lies in the desired range, no padding is needed; otherwise, an explicit larger count can be computed that achieves the desired range. Based on this lemma, the earlier corollary can be improved to give a tighter bound on the achievable distance range, and the padding technique extends directly to the general double-integer reduction case.

For a pinwheel instance with three distinct numbers, if the density is smaller than five-sixths, there exists a pinwheel schedule satisfying separation requirements for all three frequency classes. The proof for the first class follows prior work; for the second and third classes, the proof proceeds by direct manipulation of the placement formulas together with the known floor and ceiling inequalities, ultimately bounding the relevant difference from below in terms of the task counts and the density.

Similarly, for the PinFair reduction, given a pinwheel instance and a fairness weight defined for each frequency class in terms of task count, if the instance is schedulable by PinFair, then the distances between consecutive jobs of a given frequency lie in a range whose lower bound is derived from applying floor inequalities to the earliest and latest slot formulas. This lower bound is not always encouraging on its own, since it can degenerate to zero when the task count is minimal; but as with the pinwheel double-integer reduction, padding with empty tasks can be applied to satisfy the minimum separation requirement, at the cost of searching over the possible padding amounts.

In this section we compare MinMax schedulers through simulation, evaluating acceptance ratio and jitter. The acceptance ratio of a scheduler is defined as the ratio between the number of task sets schedulable by it and the total number of task sets generated. For each scheduler, the acceptance ratio is affected by two factors: the utilization or density bound of the scheduler, and the tightness of the minimum separation requirement. Intuitively, a lower utilization ratio or a smaller minimum separation requirement results in a higher acceptance ratio. The results confirm this intuition and reveal performance differences between solutions: when density is high, only two of the schedulers continue to work, and their irregular performance as the minimum-to-maximum ratio increases results from the non-linear nature of their scheduling conditions.

A goal of introducing MinMax is obtaining jitter guarantees. We define a jitter ratio for each task as the jitter divided by the average distance between consecutive jobs of that task. As the minimum separation approaches the maximum distance, the jitter ratio decreases gradually; at low density this effect is less pronounced. Comparing schedulers directly, the pinwheel-based scheduler achieves the lowest jitter, since more-less scheduling maximizes the period and thus yields smaller deadlines than earliest-deadline-first scheduling, giving it a lower jitter ratio in turn; the fairness-based scheduler, which only considers average-time fairness rather than minimizing the distance between execution and release, exhibits the highest jitter ratio among those compared.

We also examine the effect of padding with empty tasks, used in the double-integer pinwheel reduction and in the fairness-based reduction. While the density ratio is high there is no room for padding, so acceptance ratios coincide; but when bandwidth remains, padding substantially improves the acceptance ratio. With padding, the jitter ratio of the pinwheel scheduler drops close to zero; for the fairness-based scheduler, padding is necessary only when the unpadded task set is not schedulable, so schedulable sets show the same jitter ratio whether or not padding is applied, though greedy padding beyond the minimum needed increases jitter and is therefore not recommended.

We apply the unit-time MinMax model to a realistic fieldbus process control system and show that it outperforms current solutions. We compare a traditional half-half scheduling principle, in which a sensor's period is set to half that of its corresponding controller, against two MinMax-based schedulers. Both MinMax schedulers outperform the half-half principle in every test, because the half-half principle results in a much higher utilization ratio without a corresponding gain in control error. For the pinwheel-based scheduler, the utilization ratio is only about sixty percent of the half-half principle's, which could greatly save energy in wireless-based process control systems.

In this paper we examine the issue of controlling the sampling intervals of sensors in industrial process control systems such as those based on fieldbus. In contrast to traditional approaches, we do not require the sampling intervals to be strictly periodic, and we study a more relevant scheduling model with both maximum distance and minimum separation constraints. This model more closely reflects the needs of real-world industrial automation applications. We look at its basic properties and reduce the associated scheduling problem to traditional task models at the expense of some loss of schedulability, measured by an acceptance ratio in our experimental evaluation. The advantage of our approach from a practical standpoint is that we can reuse well-established and rich scheduling methods while also improving the scheduling results, such as jitter guarantees. Most of the reductions are efficient. We compare these solutions by simulation to evaluate acceptance ratio and jitter ratio, and finally apply the model to simulate a typical process control system, showing that our schedulers can outperform traditional schedulers by reducing utilization ratio with little increase in control error.
