The main result of this section is the following: Theorem 4.1 Let (M, ∂M ) be a triangulated compact P L 3-manifold with t tetrahedra, let K be an unknotted curve embedded in Interior (M ), and let ∂R K be the peripheral torus of K in the second barycentric subdivision of M . Then 15 there exists a P L closed curve K2 in ∂R K which is a longitude for ∂R K in MK such that: (i). There is an embedded P L annulus S in the solid torus RK whose two boundary components are K2 and K, and which consists of at most 21858t triangles. (ii). There is an isotopy from K2 to K across S that consists of at most 21858t+1 elementary moves. Remarks. The closed curve K2 is embedded in ∂R K but generally does not lie in its 1-skeleton. In the special case that ( M, ∂M ) is P L-homeomorphic to a triangulated convex polyhedron in R3, as occurs with a standard knot, the exponential bounds in (i), (ii) can be improved to bounds pol ynomial in t, as we indicate after the proof. The proof of Theorem 4.1 proceeds in three steps. We ﬁrst cons truct a closed curve α embedded in the 1-skeleton of RK which is parallel to the core K, i.e. its homology class [ α ] in H1(∂R K, Z) has intersection number ± 1 with a meridian [µ ]. We next observe that the homology class in H1(∂R K, Z) of a longitude K2 is necessarily [K2] = ± ([α ] + k[µ ]) (4.9) for some integer k. The second step is to bound |k|by an exponential function of t. The third step is to obtain K2 from α by adding k twists to α near a ﬁxed meridian µ . Given K2, we construct a triangulated annular surface S embedded in RK which lies in the 2-skeleton of RK except in a “collar” of one meridian disk, into which the k twists are inserted. All vertices of the triangulated annul us S lie on its boundary ∂S = K2 ∪ K. Finally we obtain an isotopy giving the elementary move bound by pulling K across the triangles in S to K2.
