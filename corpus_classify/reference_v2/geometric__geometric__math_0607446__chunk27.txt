Other compact two-point homogeneous spaces The key property underlying our proofs is the two-point homo geneity of the sphere. Recall that a metric space is two-point homogeneous if for each r > 0, its isometry group acts transitively on ordered pairs of poi nts at distance r. In this section we generalize Theorem 1.2 to all compact, conne cted two-point homogeneous spaces. This generalization is parallel to the ge neral setting for linear programming bounds in [CS, Lev2, Lev3]. It seems plausible t hat our results also generalize to the case of discrete two-point homogeneous sp aces (see Chapter 9 of [CS] for examples), but we have not investigated that possib ility. Let X be a compact, connected two-point homogeneous space with mo re than one point. Wang proved in [Wa] that, up to changing to an equiv alent metric (i.e., applying an invertible function to the metric), X is one of the following possibilities: a sphere, a real projective space, a complex projectiv e space, a quaternionic projective space, or the octonionic projective plane, each with its standard metric. In other words, we use the round metric on the sphere, the Fubi ni-Study metric on real, complex, or quaternionic projective space, and the F4-invariant metric on OP2 (up to scaling, they are the unique two-point homogeneous Ri emannian metrics on these spaces). The octonionic projective plane OP2 may be the least familiar of these possibilities; it cannot be deﬁned as O3 \ {0} modulo the action of O∗ because of the nonassociativity of the octonions ( O∗ is not a group and does not act on O3). However, it can be deﬁned using the exceptional Jordan algebra o r as F4/ Spin(9). There is no such space as OP3, because OP2 does not satisfy the Desargues theorem. See [Ba] for more details on octonionic projective geometry. Normalize the metric d on X (the geodesic distance with respect to the Riemannian metric) so that the greatest distance between point s is π . Just as we use inner products to measure distances between points on spher es, it is natural to use cos d(x, y ) instead of d(x, y ). In RPn, CPn, or HPn this function is easy to calculate: if we represent x and y by unit vectors in Rn+1, Cn+1, or Hn+1, respectively, then cos d(x, y ) = 2 |⟨x, y ⟩|2 − 1, where the brackets denote the standard Hermitian form over R, C, or H.
