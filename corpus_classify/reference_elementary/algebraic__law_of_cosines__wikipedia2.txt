The law of cosines can be proven algebraically from the law of sines and a few standard trigonometric identities. To start, the three angles of a triangle sum to a straight angle, so α + β + γ = π radians. Thus by the angle sum identities for sine and cosine,

sin γ = sin(π − γ) = sin(α + β) = sin α cos β + cos α sin β,
cos γ = −cos(π − γ) = −cos(α + β) = sin α sin β − cos α cos β.

Squaring the first of these identities, then substituting cos α cos β = sin α sin β − cos γ from the second, and finally replacing cos²α + sin²α = cos²β + sin²β = 1, the Pythagorean trigonometric identity, we have:

sin²γ = (sin α cos β + cos α sin β)²
      = sin²α cos²β + 2 sin α sin β cos α cos β + cos²α sin²β
      = sin²α cos²β + 2 sin α sin β (sin α sin β − cos γ) + cos²α sin²β
      = sin²α (cos²β + sin²β) + sin²β (cos²α + sin²α) − 2 sin α sin β cos γ
      = sin²α + sin²β − 2 sin α sin β cos γ.

The law of sines holds that

a / sin α = b / sin β = c / sin γ = k,

so to prove the law of cosines, we multiply both sides of the previous identity by k²:

sin²γ · c²/sin²γ = sin²α · a²/sin²α + sin²β · b²/sin²β − 2 sin α sin β cos γ · ab/(sin α sin β),
c² = a² + b² − 2ab cos γ.

This concludes the proof.
